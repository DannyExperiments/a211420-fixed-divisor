\section{Statement}

For $n,r,k\in\N$, put
\[
 A_{\rm num}(n)=(8n)!\,n!,\qquad
 A_{\rm den}(n)=(4n)!(3n)!(2n)!,
\]
\[
 D_{k,r}(n)=\prod_{i=1}^{r}(kn+i),\qquad
 L_r=\operatorname{lcm}(1,2,\ldots,8r-1).
\]

\begin{theorem}[Strengthened A211420 divisibility]\label{thm:main}
For every $n\geq0$, every $r\geq1$, and every $k\in\{1,2,3\}$,
\[
 A_{\rm den}(n)\mid A_{\rm num}(n)
\]
and
\[
 D_{k,r}(n)A_{\rm den}(n)\mid L_r^r A_{\rm num}(n).
\]
Consequently, with the exact integer quotient
\[
 a_n=\frac{A_{\rm num}(n)}{A_{\rm den}(n)},
\]
one has $L_r^r>0$ and
\[
 D_{k,r}(n)\mid L_r^r a_n.
\]
\end{theorem}

\section{Prior work and contribution boundary}

The integrality of $a_n$ was already known from Bober's classification:
it is the $a=4$, $b=1$ specialization of his infinite family of integral
factorial ratios~\cite[Theorem~1.2, family (8)]{Bober2009}. The present
Lean development formalizes this integrality rather than assuming it.

The weaker assertion that, for each fixed $k\in\{1,2,3\}$ and $r\geq1$,
some positive constant $C(k,r)$ exists follows from Landau's multivariate
factorial-ratio criterion after a two-variable specialization; for example,
\[
 C(k,r)=((8r)!)^r
\]
is sufficient. This is background identified in the accompanying audits and
is not the formally verified contribution claimed here. The result proved and
formalized here is the stronger explicit bound
\[
 C(k,r)=L_r^r.
\]
No claim of minimality or historical priority is made. The searches conducted
found no prior source for this full explicit strengthening.

Two earlier results give exact or closely related special cases. Sun's
Theorem~1.3(ii), specialized with his parameter $k=4$, yields
\[
 (3n+1)\mid 5a_n
\]
for every $n$~\cite{Sun2012}. Yang's Theorem~1, specialized with $a=4$ and
$b=1$, yields
\[
 (2n+1)(2n+3)\mid 99a_n
\]
for every positive $n$~\cite{Yang2015}. Yang's second factor is $2n+3$,
so this is related to the earlier odd-progression problem rather than the
consecutive $r=2$ product in Theorem~\ref{thm:main}.

\section{Ordinary mathematical proof}

For a prime $p$, initially interpret $v_p$ as the valuation on positive
rational numbers. Define the one-periodic Landau step function
\[
 \Delta(x)=\floor{8x}+\floor{x}-\floor{4x}-\floor{3x}-\floor{2x}.
\]
The coefficient balance $8+1-4-3-2=0$ gives
$\Delta(x+1)=\Delta(x)$. On $[0,1)$ the relevant breakpoints are
\[
0,\frac18,\frac14,\frac13,\frac38,\frac12,
\frac58,\frac23,\frac34,\frac78,1.
\]
Direct evaluation on the intervening half-open intervals gives
\[
\Delta(x)=1
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\{x\}\in
\left[\frac18,\frac13\right)
\cup\left[\frac38,\frac12\right)
\cup\left[\frac58,\frac23\right)
\cup\left[\frac78,1\right),
\]
and $\Delta(x)=0$ otherwise. In particular, $\Delta\geq0$.

Legendre's formula gives the finite identity
\[
 v_p\!\left(\frac{A_{\rm num}(n)}{A_{\rm den}(n)}\right)
 =\sum_{j\geq1}\Delta\!\left(\frac{n}{p^j}\right).
\]
Indeed, all summands vanish when $p^j>8n$. Every prime valuation of the
positive rational factorial quotient is therefore nonnegative. It follows
that $A_{\rm den}(n)\mid A_{\rm num}(n)$ and $a_n$ is a positive integer.

For a prime power $q=p^j$, define
\[
 N_q=\#\{i\in\{1,\ldots,r\}:q\mid kn+i\}.
\]
Then
\[
 v_p(D_{k,r}(n))=\sum_{j\geq1}N_{p^j},
\]
again with only finitely many nonzero terms.

Suppose first that $q\geq8r$. Then $q>r$, so at most one integer among
$kn+1,\ldots,kn+r$ is divisible by $q$ and $N_q\leq1$. If
$q\mid kn+i$, write $kn+i=mq$. Then
\[
 \frac nq=\frac mk-\frac{i}{kq},
 \qquad 0<\frac{i}{kq}\leq\frac1{8k}.
\]
For $k=1$, the fractional part of $n/q$ lies in $[7/8,1)$. For $k=2$,
according as $m$ is even or odd, it lies in $[15/16,1)$ or
$[7/16,1/2)$. For $k=3$, according to $m$ modulo $3$, it lies in
\[
[23/24,1),\qquad [7/24,1/3),\qquad [5/8,2/3).
\]
Every displayed interval is contained in the support of $\Delta$. The
included left endpoints cover the equality case $q=8r$ and $i=r$.
Consequently
\[
 N_q\leq\Delta(n/q)\qquad(q\geq8r).
\]

For $q=p^j<8r$, use the elementary bound $N_q\leq r$. The exact definition
of $L_r$ gives
\[
 v_p(L_r)=\#\{j\geq1:p^j<8r\}.
\]
Therefore, for every prime $p$,
\begin{align*}
v_p(D_{k,r}(n))
 &=\sum_{p^j<8r}N_{p^j}+\sum_{p^j\geq8r}N_{p^j}\\
 &\leq r\,v_p(L_r)+\sum_{p^j\geq8r}\Delta(n/p^j)\\
 &\leq r\,v_p(L_r)+\sum_{j\geq1}\Delta(n/p^j)\\
 &=v_p(L_r^r a_n).
\end{align*}
The second inequality uses $\Delta\geq0$ for the omitted small powers.
Prime-by-prime comparison proves $D_{k,r}(n)\mid L_r^r a_n$.
Finally, $L_r$ is a nonzero least common multiple, so $L_r^r>0$.
This proves Theorem~\ref{thm:main}.

\section{Scope and provenance boundary}

The theorem displayed above is kernel-checked. Finite computations were used
only for falsification and diagnosis, not as proof. Four AI-generated
adversarial audit records distributed with the repository are not human peer
review. No claim of historical priority, minimality of $L_r^r$, or formal
verification of additional proposed variants is made.

The searches supplied with this project found no prior source for the exact
$L_r^r$ strengthening, but bounded searches cannot certify originality. The
integrality background belongs to the classical Landau factorial-ratio
framework; see Bober's classification and the work of Rodriguez-Villegas.
